1.1.2 Homogeneous Coordinates¶

Translation cannot be represented in matrix form and we don't want translation to be a special case. So we introduce the concept of homogeneous coordinates.

We add a third coordinate(w-coordinate)

2D point \(\rightarrow\) \((x,y,1)^T\) 2D vector \(\rightarrow\) \((x,y,0)^T\) (Let vector do not change when translation happens)

Meanwhile, valid operation if w-coordinate of result is 1/0

  • vector+vector=vector
  • point-point=vector
  • point+vector=point

However, point+point is meaningless at some point. In homogeneous coordinates,

\(\left( \begin{matrix} x\\y\\w\end{matrix} \right) \tag{2}\)is the \(2D\) point\(\left( \begin{matrix} x/w\\y/w\end{matrix} \right) \tag{2}\),\(w\neq0\). Therefore point+point means the midpoint.

1.2 Viewing transformation¶

1.2.1 View/Camera Transformation¶

We need to define the camera first.

  • Position \(\vec e\)
  • Look at / gaze direction \(\hat g\)
  • Up direction \(\hat t\)

To let the problem be easy, we can move the camera and the object together. Usually, we transform the camera to the origin, up at Y, look at -Z. Let the transform matrix be \(M_{view}\).

How to get \(M_{view}\) in math?

  1. translate \(\vec e\) to origin then rotate g to -Z, t to Y, (g*t) to X
  2. \(M_{view}=R_{view}T_{view}\)

  3. \(T_{view}= \left[ \begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & -x_e \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -y_e\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -z_e \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{matrix} \right]\)

  4. \(R^{-1}_{view}= \left[ \begin{matrix} x_{\hat g \times \hat t} & x_t & x_{-g} & 0 \\ y_{\hat g \times \hat t} & y_t & y_{-g} & 0\\ z_{\hat g \times \hat t} & z_t & z_{-g} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{matrix} \right]\)\(\Rightarrow\) \(R_{view}= \left[ \begin{matrix} x_{\hat g \times \hat t} & y_{\hat g \times \hat t} & z_{\hat g \times \hat t} & 0 \\ x_t & y_t & z_t & 0\\ x_{-g} & y_{-g}& z_{-g} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{matrix} \right]\)

1.2.2 Projection transformation¶

Perspective projection vs. orthographic projection

Orthographic Projection

We can drop Z coordinate and translate and scale the resulting rectangle to \([-1,1]^2\)

However, in general, we want to map a cuboid \([l,r]\times[b,t]\times[f,n]\) to the “canonical” cube \([-1,1]^3\)

The transformation matrix: Translate first, then scale

\(M_{ortho}=\left[ \begin{matrix} \frac{2}{r-l} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{2}{t-b }& 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \frac{2}{n-f} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{matrix} \right]\left[ \begin{matrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & -\frac{r+l}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -\frac{t+b}{2}\\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -\frac{n+f}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{matrix} \right]\)

Perspective Projection

Perspective Projection is common in Computer Graphics and so on, featuring that further objects are smaller and parallel lines not parallel.

How to do perspective projection?

  1. “squish” the frustum in to cuboid(\(M_{persp\rightarrow ortho}\))
  2. do orthographic projection

According to the similar triangle ,\(x'=\frac{n}{z}x\) \(y'=\frac{n}{z}y\)

so the ”squish” projection does this \(M^{(4\times 4)}_{persp\rightarrow ortho}\left(\begin{matrix}x\\y\\z\\1 \end{matrix}\right)=\left(\begin{matrix}nx\\ny\\unknown\\z \end{matrix}\right)\)

then we can get \(M^{(4\times 4)}_{persp\rightarrow ortho}=\left(\begin{matrix}n&0&0&0\\0&n&0&0\\?&?&?&?\\0&0&1&0 \end{matrix}\right)\)

Through observation, we find any point on the near plane will not change and any point’z on the far plane also not change. So we can use this two special to solve the third row of the matrix.

\(\left(\begin{matrix}x\\y\\n\\1\end{matrix}\right)\Rightarrow \left(\begin{matrix}x\\y\\n\\1\end{matrix}\right) \equiv \left(\begin{matrix}nx\\ny\\n^2\\n\end{matrix}\right)\) , \(\left(\begin{matrix}0\\0\\f\\1\end{matrix}\right)\Rightarrow \left(\begin{matrix}0\\0\\f\\1\end{matrix}\right) \equiv \left(\begin{matrix}0\\0\\f^2\\f\end{matrix}\right)\)

by the first equation we know that the third row must be \(\left(\begin{matrix}0&0&A&B\end{matrix}\right)\)

so we can the equation set \(\begin{cases} An+B =n^2\\Af+B=f^2\end{cases}\) \(\rightarrow\begin{cases} A=n+f\\b=-nf\end{cases}\)

Which planes is closer to the point in frustum that is not on the n and f planes after the squish?

f planes

How to define such a frustum?

  • define width and height \(\rightarrow\) Aspect ratio = width/height
  • define vertical field of view: \(fovY\)

it is easy to know: \(tan\frac{fovY}{2}=\frac{t}{\abs{n}}\) \(aspect=\frac{r}{t}\)
